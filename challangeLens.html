Lens of Exploration & Discovery

Back

The whole game is based around exploring your family’s new house in order to discover how they’re lives have changed. For example, through the mess of papers on your father’s desk that all have to do with his next book and you can tell how overwhelmed he is by it. You can find tons of crumpled pieces of paper in the trash of failed ideas he’s had, showing that he might be suffering from writer’s block. You see on his bulletin board that along with all his other ideas he wrote in big bold letters “you can do better.”

From the letters you find hidden in his cabinets you find out that his last and second book didn’t do so well and that his next book is being seen as his last chance to prove himself to his publishers. You later find a different letter from his father explaining how disappointed he is in the first book he wrote saying that “you can do better.” This helps to explain the failure of the second book as his publisher complains that it was overwritten and that he needs to pace himself, showing that he might have burnt himself out in order to impress his father. Later on you can find a pamphlet for a marriage counselor in his room which can indicate that his work has pushed him and his wife away from each other.

All of these little environmental details add up to a surprisingly vivid portrait of a tortured author who’s pushed himself too hard and now feels like he’s failed at everything, isolating those he most cares out of what might be shame, but undoubtedly also to keep trying to impress his father, but feels unable to do what everyone is depending on him for because of his writer’s block. It’s startling how much depth this game can give to its main characters without ever letting you meet them and it does it all by letting you explore and discover all this for yourself, letting you put the pieces together on your own, making it feel even more satisfying when you find everything and feel like you really understand everyone in this house.
